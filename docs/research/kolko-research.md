# Research Brief: Jon Kolko — Design Synthesis, Abductive Thinking & Sensemaking

> **Source**: Primarily from "Abductive Thinking and Sensemaking: The Drivers of Design Synthesis" (Design Issues, MIT, 2010), "Exposing the Magic of Design" (Oxford UP, 2011), and "Information Architecture: Synthesis Techniques for the Muddy Middle" (2008).  
> **Date**: 2026-04-16  
> **Purpose**: Extract concrete frameworks for a cartography practice — the art of mapping invisible structures across domains.

---

## 1. Design Synthesis: The Core Process

### Definition
Synthesis is the act of **organizing, manipulating, pruning, and filtering gathered data into a cohesive structure for information building**. It's the bridge between raw research and design action — the phase where chaos becomes structured insight.

### The Three Chasms (Data → Information → Knowledge → Wisdom)

Kolko maps synthesis across four progressive stages, separated by three "chasms" that must be actively crossed:

| Stage | What It Is | The Chasm Before It |
|-------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Data** | Raw observations, transcripts, photos, artifacts | — |
| **Information** | Organized, related data with emerging patterns | Chasm 1: Making meaning out of data |
| **Knowledge** | Actionable understanding; design constraints identified | Chasm 2: Converting information to knowledge |
| **Wisdom** | Strategic, contextualized insight that drives innovation | Chasm 3: Applying knowledge to generate wisdom |

### The Four Steps of Chasm 1 (Making Meaning Out of Data)

These are the **most actionable** steps Kolko describes:

1. **Externalize the entire process** — Move data out of digital silos (laptops, folders) onto physical walls. The artificial constraints of file systems limit your ability to see relationships. A wall + sticky notes + marker = the foundational synthesis tool.
2. **Use visual design to clean up the mess** — Apply craft: consistent layouts, squared corners, rewritten key points. Multiple "cleanup sweeps" throughout the process. The act of tidying IS sensemaking.
3. **Organize to produce semantic relationships** — Move items physically. Place related things next to each other. Don't seek "right" relationships — seek "good" ones. Duplicate items that belong in multiple groups. Abandon and rearrange groupings freely.
4. **Make relationships explicit in plain language** — Label groupings. The label captures both literal and implied meaning. The act of naming forces clarity.

### Key Insight for Cartography
> "The designer needs the organization in order to gain a complete picture of the design space; they then draw conclusions, and the synthesis wall becomes unnecessary. It has served its purpose in delineating the design space."

**The map's purpose is to change the mapper.** Once you've internalized the structure, the external artifact has done its job. This is deeply relevant to lattice-building — the vault exists to transform the cartographer's understanding, not as an end in itself.

## 2. Abductive Thinking

### The Three Forms of Reasoning

| Type | Logic | Example |
|------|-------|---------|
| **Deduction** | Rule + Case → Result | All swans are birds. This is a swan. → This is a bird. |
| **Induction** | Cases → Rule (probable) | Every swan I've seen is white. → All swans are probably white. |
| **Abduction** | Rule + Result → *Possible* Case | Birds have feathers. This has feathers. → This *might* be a bird. |

### What Makes Abduction Different
- Deduction proves. Induction probabilizes. **Abduction hypothesizes.**
- It's the "best explanation" reasoning — given incomplete data, what story *could* account for what we see?
- It's inherently **creative and risky**. It generates new possibilities rather than confirming existing ones.
- Peirce (originator): abduction is "the only logical operation which introduces any new idea."

### How Designers Practice Abduction
- They gather data (observation, ethnography)
- They notice **gaps, tensions, and contradictions** in the data
- They leap to a **provisional frame** — a story that *could* explain what they see
- They test that frame against reality and iterate

### For Cartography Practice
Abduction is exactly what a cartographer does when mapping invisible structures: you see fragments across domains, notice a pattern that *could* connect them, and build a provisional map. The map is the abductive hypothesis. It's not proven — it's the best current explanation for what you're seeing.

## 3. Sensemaking Framework

### Core Definition (from Klein, Moon & Hoffman)
> "A motivated, continuous effort to understand connections (which can be among people, places, and events) in order to anticipate their trajectories and act effectively."

### Dervin's Complement
> Sense-making reconceptualizes "factizing" as one of the useful verbings humans use to make sense of their worlds.

### Kolko's Synthesis of Sensemaking

**Sensemaking is internal and personal. Synthesis is external and collaborative.**

The progression:
1. Individual sensemaking — you're integrating experiences into your understanding
2. Externalization — you "get it out" to make it tangible, discussable, rejectable
3. Spatial organization — you arrange the externalized data to reveal structure
4. Pattern identification — relationships emerge from proximity and grouping
5. Labeling — you name what you see, making the implicit explicit
6. The artifact becomes unnecessary — you've internalized the structure

### The "Magic" Problem
Kolko identifies that synthesis appears magical because:
- It's done privately (in the head, on scratch paper)
- The input → output connection is invisible
- Even designers can't always articulate *why* their insights are valuable
- Clients see the result but not the process

**The fix**: Make synthesis visible, documented, repeatable. The "magic" is a failure of communication, not a feature of genius.

### For Cartography Practice
This is directly why the lattice must be visible. If your knowledge mapping happens only in your head, it's magical (in the bad sense — unexaminable, untestable, uncommunicable). The Obsidian vault IS the synthesis wall. Every atom is a sticky note. Every MOC is a grouping label.

## 4. Synthesis Methods (Concrete Tools)

Kolko identifies several specific methods from "Exposing the Magic of Design":

### 4.1 Affinity Diagramming
- Write each data point on a separate note
- Group notes by perceived similarity (not pre-defined categories)
- Name each group
- Iterate: move notes, split groups, merge groups
- **Actionable rule**: Don't seek "correct" groupings. Seek "good" groupings that reveal non-obvious connections.

### 4.2 Concept Mapping
- Nodes = concepts, edges = relationships
- Start from a central question or problem statement
- Expand outward, linking related concepts
- Label the edges (not just the nodes) — the relationship IS the insight
- **Key principle**: Concept maps are graphical representations of mental models. They make thinking visible.

### 4.3 Process Flow Diagramming
- Map sequential steps in a user journey or process
- Identify breakdowns, pain points, and moments of delight
- Look for gaps between intended and actual process

### 4.4 Insight Combination
- Take two or more discrete research findings
- Force them together to see what emerges
- The combination itself is the generative act
- **This is explicitly abductive** — you're hypothesizing that the combination reveals something real

### 4.5 Reframing
- Deliberately change the frame through which you view data
- Same data + different frame = different insights
- The frame is not neutral — it determines what you can see

## 5. Visual Thinking & Externalization

### Why Externalize?
- Working memory is limited — you literally cannot hold enough data in your head
- Digital tools impose artificial structures (file hierarchies, database schemas)
- Physical space allows free manipulation, proximity-based grouping, and gestalt seeing
- Externalized ideas become "real" — discussable, challengeable, improvable

### The Wall as Instrument
Kolko repeatedly emphasizes: **a big wall, a marker, and lots of sticky notes** are the most common and effective synthesis tools. Not software. Not databases. A wall.

The wall provides:
- **Spatial freedom** — no artificial grid or hierarchy
- **Simultaneity** — you can see everything at once
- **Physical manipulation** — moving a note is a cognitive act
- **Scale** — the mess is visible in its entirety

### Translation to Digital Cartography
The Obsidian lattice must replicate these properties:
- Tags and links = proximity (things near each other in concept-space)
- MOCs = grouping labels
- Atoms = sticky notes
- Bidirectional links = the "edges" in a concept map
- The graph view = the wall

## 6. Frameworks for Making Invisible Structures Visible

### 6.1 The Spatialization Principle
**Move data from sequential/digital storage into spatial arrangement.** The spatial arrangement IS the analysis. Where you place something relative to other things is a claim about relationship.

### 6.2 The Labeling Principle
**Naming a grouping is an act of meaning-making.** The label doesn't describe what's there — it creates what's there. A good label makes implicit structure explicit.

### 6.3 The Duplication Principle
**If a datum belongs to multiple groups, duplicate it.** Don't force a single taxonomy. Real phenomena are multi-faceted. Multiple simultaneous groupings = richer understanding.

### 6.4 The Iterative Abandonment Principle
**Be willing to rearrange and abandon earlier groupings.** The first organization is not the final one. Each rearrangement is a new abductive hypothesis.

### 6.5 The Progressive Escape
**Synthesis is a "progressive escape from the mess."** You don't organize once — you organize, see structure, internalize it, and then the external artifact becomes less necessary. The goal is transformation of the mapper, not preservation of the map.

### 6.6 The "Good" vs "Right" Distinction
In synthesis, there is no single correct answer. The question is not "is this group right?" but "is this grouping *productive*?" Does it reveal something that wasn't visible before? Productive groupings are those that are multi-faceted, complex, and culturally rooted.

## 7. Implications for Cartography Practice

### Direct Translations

| Kolko Concept | Lattice Equivalent |
|--------------|-------------------|
| Synthesis wall | Obsidian vault / graph view |
| Sticky notes | Atomic notes in `atlas/` |
| Grouping labels | MOC titles |
| Concept maps | Bidirectional `[[]]` links + graph |
| Externalization | Writing atoms instead of "mental notes" |
| Abductive leap | Creating a new atom that connects disparate domains |
| The three chasms | Data → Atom → MOC → Integrated understanding |
| "Progressive escape" | Once you understand the lattice, individual atoms matter less |

### Core Practices to Adopt

1. **Always externalize.** If you think you see a connection, write it as an atom. Unwritten = invisible = untestable.
2. **Label edges, not just nodes.** When linking atoms, name the relationship. "[[X]] contradicts [[Y]]" is more valuable than "[[X]] → [[Y]]."
3. **Embrace abductive leaps.** Don't wait for proof. Create provisional atoms that hypothesize connections. Mark them as speculative. Let reality test them.
4. **Practice reframing.** Take existing atoms and reorganize them under a new MOC. Same data, different frame = new insights.
5. **Duplicate rather than force-fit.** If a concept belongs in multiple MOCs, link it from all of them. Don't create a single "correct" taxonomy.
6. **Accept the mess.** The lattice will always be partially messy. That mess IS the thinking. Cleanup is part of the process, not a precondition.

### The Kolko Test for Any Lattice Atom
> Can someone else see the connection between this atom and the data/experience that produced it? If not, the synthesis is still "magic" — and not in the good way.
