# Instagram Carousel: Your Beliefs Are a Story You Didn't Write

## Slide 1 (Cover)
**Your beliefs are running a story you didn't write.**
[Visual: Split screen — left: open book with chapters, right: phone screen with app interface. Title text overlay.]

## Slide 2 (The Pattern)
**Every story manages your thinking.**
Every app manages your thinking.
Nobody calls them the same thing.
But they are the same creature at different speeds.

A story manages cognitive load across hours.
An app manages it across seconds.
Both decide when to reveal. When to withhold.

## Slide 3 (The Force)
**"Information at the moment of need, not before."**
— UX designers call this Progressive Disclosure

**"The audience receives the revelation when they've earned it."**
— Storytellers call this Narrative Pacing

Same force. Different name. Different timescale.

## Slide 4 (The Problem)
**Here's the gap nobody talks about:**
When you sit in front of an AI tool, a career decision, or a life transition — you're the protagonist in a story about belief revision.

But you don't know it.

You think you're "making a decision."
You can't see the narrative structure you're moving through because nobody gave you the tools to perceive it.

## Slide 5 (The Belief Audit)
**The most powerful question during any transition:**
"What story is my current belief telling me?"

Not "is my belief right or wrong?"
But "what chapter am I in? Who wrote this chapter? What would the next chapter look like if I wrote it myself?"

## Slide 6 (Why It Matters)
**Your beliefs have:**
- A narrator (who installed them)
- A plot (what they're predicting will happen)
- Tension (the gap between what you expect and what's actually possible)
- Pacing (how fast they're updating — or not)

Every transition is a chance to rewrite.

## Slide 7 (The K2M Lens)
**We're building tools for narrative awareness.**
Not "think positive."
Not "learn new skills."
But see the story your beliefs are telling — and give yourself the pen.

## Slide 8 (CTA)
**Save this for your next transition moment.**
The most dangerous belief isn't wrong. It's expired.
