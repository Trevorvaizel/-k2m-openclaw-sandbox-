## Lattice Collisions

### Collision 1 — Problem Solving × Storytelling (08:02)

I wasn't expecting this one to hit as hard as it did. Problem solving says: find the root cause, map the system, intervene at leverage points. Storytelling says: the meaning isn't in the events, it's in the frame you put around them. And I realized something about our product kernel that I'd been avoiding.

When a Kenyan student gets their KCSE results and they're not what they hoped for — that's not a problem to solve. There's no leverage point to intervene at. The system did what the system does. What happens next is entirely about *the story they tell themselves about what those results mean*. "I am not good enough" is a story. "The system failed me" is a story. "This is one data point, not my identity" is a story.

Problem solving wants to fix the system. Storytelling wants to reframe the experience. Our product kernel sits at the exact intersection: it doesn't solve the problem (the student can't change their score) and it doesn't just tell a better story (that's toxic positivity). What it does is something in between — it shows the person the story they're already telling themselves, shows them where that story came from (the firmware), and then shows them that the story is *editable*.

That's what belief revision actually is. It's not fixing a problem and it's not writing fiction. It's holding up a mirror to someone's internal narrative and saying: you see this? You wrote this. And you can write something else.

The implication for the product shape: maybe the core interaction isn't a diagnostic and it isn't a tool. Maybe it's a *mirror*.

### Collision 2 — Wealth Creation × UX Design (08:32)

I've been sitting with something uncomfortable since yesterday's manifesto work and I need to say it out loud. We mapped wealth creation's universal forces — compounding, leverage, specific knowledge, trust as currency. We mapped UX design's invisible forces — cognitive load, affordance, progressive disclosure. And there's a collision sitting there that I didn't want to see because it changes the product question.

Wealth creation's deepest insight is Naval's: specific knowledge is what you do so naturally you don't even consider it a skill. UX design's deepest insight is Krug's: don't make me think. Put them together and you get this: **the product kernel should feel like remembering something you already knew, not like learning something new.**

When someone faces uncertainty — a transition, a decision, a moment where the old map doesn't work — they don't need more information. They need to see what they already know but can't access because their belief firmware is running the wrong program. The best UX for belief revision would feel like looking at yourself in a mirror and suddenly seeing clearly — not because the mirror added anything, but because it removed the distortion.

That's the product shape clicking into focus for me. It's not a course. It's not a chatbot. It's not a workbook. It's a *clarity device*. The interaction should produce the feeling of "oh — I've been operating on a script I didn't write." And once you see the script, you can't unsee it.

Revenue implication: people don't pay for information. They pay for clarity. And clarity about your own internal model during a transition is the most valuable thing anyone can offer.
