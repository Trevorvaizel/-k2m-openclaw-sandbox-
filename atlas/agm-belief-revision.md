# AGM Belief Revision

> *The formal logic of how a rational agent should update beliefs when encountering new information.*

## Definition

AGM theory (Alchourrón, Gärdenfors, Makinson, 1985) defines three operations on a belief state:

1. **Expansion** — Add a new belief to the existing set (no conflict removal)
2. **Revision** — Add a new belief *and* remove anything that contradicts it (maintain consistency)
3. **Contraction** — Remove a belief without adding anything new

The theory assumes a belief state is a logically closed set of sentences. Revision is the most important operation — it's what happens when reality gives you information that doesn't fit your current model.

## Invisible Mechanism

Most people never perform true *revision*. They expand (add beliefs) or contract (ignore disconfirming evidence). Genuine revision — holding a belief, encountering contradiction, and restructuring — is cognitively expensive and emotionally threatening. This connects directly to [[belief-gap|The Belief Gap]]: the distance between current and needed belief is not just informational but structural.

## Cross-Domain Manifestations

- **Marketing**: A customer who believes "I can't afford this" receiving evidence that the cost of inaction is higher → revision required
- **Education**: A student whose [[kenyan-student-belief-artifacts|Kenyan student belief artifacts]] have encoded "I am a B student" encountering a subject where they excel → identity-threatening revision
- **Relationships**: [[moc-relationship-cartography|Relationship dynamics]] where a partner's behavior contradicts the narrative you've built about them

## Diagnostic Question

*When was the last time I revised a core belief — not added to it, not ignored contradiction, but actually restructured?*

## Trap

The AGM theory assumes rational agents. Humans are not rational agents. We perform pseudo-revision: we appear to update while secretly maintaining the original structure. This is [[constraint-catalyst|constraint]] maintenance disguised as growth.
