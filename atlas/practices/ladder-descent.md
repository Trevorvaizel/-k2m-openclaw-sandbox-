# Ladder Descent

> Every conclusion is an inference. The discipline is to climb back down.

## Definition
The Ladder Descent is a structured inference-tracing practice drawn from Argyris's Ladder of Inference. After any significant interpretation, diagnosis, or force-naming, trace the reasoning backward: state the conclusion, then ask what data was selected, what was omitted, what assumption was made in the interpretation step, and where a different data selection would have led.

## The Practice
1. State the conclusion or force-name you reached.
2. Ask: What data did I select to reach this? What did I observe that I am treating as evidence?
3. Ask: What did I leave out? What data was available but not included?
4. Ask: What assumption did I make in the interpretation step — between the data and the conclusion?
5. Ask: Where would a different data selection or assumption have led?

## Why This Matters
Force-naming compresses observation into a portable handle. The compression creates authority. That authority is only deserved if the inference is traceable. The Ladder Descent makes the inferential moves visible before the name freezes into established canon. It prevents the Abductive-Naming Force (Force IV) from producing elegant abstractions that are not accountable to evidence.

## Force Connections
- Activates Constant-Comparison (Force V): forces comparison of what was selected against what was available
- Activates Anti-Mimicry (Force VIII): catches the moment where inference is mistaken for data
- Supports Decommissioning (Force XI): maps that cannot survive a ladder descent are candidates for retirement

## Cadence
After any significant interpretation, diagnosis, or force-naming. Especially before treating a pattern as a force and before publishing a force as canon.

## Source
Argyris, Chris. *Overcoming Organizational Defenses* (1990). Allyn & Bacon. — the Ladder of Inference as a diagnostic instrument for making inference chains visible and auditable.
