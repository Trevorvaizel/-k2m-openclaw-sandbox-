# Associative Barrier Audit

> List the territories you know most deeply. For each, write the assumption you hold most strongly. Then identify a field where that assumption is false. Study that field briefly.

## Definition
The Associative Barrier Audit is a structured disruption practice for breaking the cognitive barriers that deep domain expertise builds. Expertise makes within-domain thinking efficient — and cross-domain collision impossible. This audit systematically exposes the practitioner to fields where their strongest assumptions are wrong.

## The Practice
1. List 2–3 territories you know most deeply.
2. For each territory, write the single assumption you hold most strongly.
3. For each assumption, identify a field where that assumption is false or inverted.
4. Study that field briefly — one week of reading is enough.
5. Note what the collision reveals about the original assumption.

## Why Associative Barriers Form
Deep expertise builds strong neural pathways — associations that bind ideas predictably. These bindings are efficient within a domain but block cross-domain collisions. The practitioner sees only what their expertise trains them to see. Johansson: "Innovation lives at the intersection." The barriers that block intersection are built by the same mastery that makes you good within a domain.

## Cadence
Quarterly. Or any time a territory feels too familiar and easy — that ease is often a warning signal of entrenched barriers.

## Source
Johansson, *The Medici Effect* (2004/2006): associative barriers as the mechanism that blocks cross-domain innovation; the audit as the operational move for breaking them. Vault source: `docs/research/johansson-research.md`.
