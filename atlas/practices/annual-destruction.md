# Annual Destruction

> Once a year, identify the cartographic concept you are most attached to and actively attempt to kill it.

## Definition
Annual Destruction is the disciplined, recurring practice of selecting your most strongly held map, model, or force name and subjecting it to adversarial review. If it survives steelmanned opposition, keep it — tagged as tested-this-year. If it does not survive, retire or narrow it.

## The Practice
1. Identify the force, model, or frame you are most attached to this year.
2. Steelman the strongest opposing evidence — not the weakest critique, the best one.
3. Test whether the map still explains failure, not just success.
4. If it survives: tag it as tested, carry it forward with higher confidence.
5. If it does not survive: retire it, narrow its scope, or mark it provisional.

## Why This Works
The Decommissioning Force operates through cognitive authority — the more beautiful and useful a map has been, the harder it is to kill. Annual Destruction turns decommissioning from a crisis (when reality forces it) into a practice (before reality forces it). It preserves the canon from becoming archaeology.

## Cadence
Once per year minimum. More frequent versions run quarterly or after any significant new territory encounter.

## Source
Munger: "Any year that you don't destroy one of your best-loved ideas is probably a wasted year." Attested in vault transcripts (Great Mental Models Vol. 1 set); attributed to *Poor Charlie's Almanack* and Berkshire annual-meeting commentary.

The steelmanning condition is also Munger's: "I'm not entitled to have an opinion on this subject unless I can state the arguments against my position better than the people in opposition."
