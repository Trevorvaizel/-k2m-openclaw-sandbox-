# Structural Differential Audit

> A map works not because it is detailed. It works because it shares the right structure with its territory.

## Definition
The Structural Differential Audit is a map-evaluation practice drawn from Korzybski's General Semantics. When you inherit or build a map, ask three questions in sequence: (1) What events or observations is this map abstracted from? (2) What characteristics of those events did the abstraction retain? (3) What characteristics did it leave out?

## The Practice
1. Identify the map or framework under audit (a force name, a stage model, a causal claim, a manifesto section).
2. Ask: What events, observations, or cases is this abstracted from? Name them specifically.
3. Ask: What characteristics of those events did the abstraction retain? What is the map actually tracking?
4. Ask: What characteristics did the abstraction leave out? This is the most important question. If you cannot answer it, you do not yet understand the map you are using.
5. Ask: Does the map share the right structure with the territory it claims to describe — or has the abstraction introduced structural distortion?

## Why This Matters
Korzybski's central operational claim: a map works not because it is detailed but because it shares structural relations with the territory. A structurally wrong map fails regardless of how detailed it is — and adding detail makes it worse by increasing confidence in a wrong structure. Every abstraction retains some characteristics and omits others. The omissions define what the map is for — and what it will systematically fail to explain. If the omissions are invisible, the failure mode is also invisible.

## Force Connections
- Operationalizes Omission-Power (Force IX): the audit makes omissions visible and named
- Activates Constant-Comparison (Force V): ask what would change if you had selected different characteristics
- Supports Decommissioning (Force XI): maps that cannot survive a structural audit are structurally wrong, not just incomplete

## Cadence
When inheriting any map or framework from another domain or practitioner. Before treating a force as settled. After any significant new territory contact that might have changed the underlying structure.

## Source
Korzybski, Alfred. *Science and Sanity: An Introduction to Non-Aristotelian Systems and General Semantics* (1933; 4th ed. 1958). Institute of General Semantics. — structural similarity as the basis of map usefulness (Part II, Chapter 4, "On Structure"); the Structural Differential as a teaching device for making abstraction levels visible.
