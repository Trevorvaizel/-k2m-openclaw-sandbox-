# Tool 3: Inversion

> Study failure paths first. Success options get clearer after the avoidable ruin becomes visible.

## Purpose
Use inversion when a goal is too vague, too crowded, or too emotionally loaded. Thinking backward turns an open-ended ambition into a concrete map of avoidable errors, zeros, and failure paths.

## Process
1. State the desired outcome.
2. Invert the question: what would reliably produce the opposite result?
3. List the failure modes, frictions, and single-point collapses.
4. Remove the highest-leverage brake or zero before adding more effort.
5. Re-run the question after each major change.

## Result
- The problem becomes smaller and more operational.
- Failure patterns become visible before they become expensive.
- Prevention and positioning replace heroic effort.

## When To Use
Use inversion when a system is competitive, when motivation has stopped working, when hidden downside can wipe out upside, or when you keep optimizing without removing the thing that is killing the result.

## Failure Mode
Treating inversion as cynicism or permanent defensiveness. The point is not to admire failure. The point is to remove the preventable collapse so the positive path has a chance to work.
