# Predictive Processing - Deep Dive

> Belief revision is hard because the brain is not waiting passively for truth. It is actively predicting, filtering, and protecting its current model.

## Definition
This deep-dive note extends [[predictive-processing]] by focusing on the machinery beneath the summary: generative models, prediction errors, precision weighting, and active inference. The core point is that beliefs are not only explicit statements; they are operating predictions that shape what the person notices and how they act.

## Pattern
- A generative model predicts what the world is like.
- Incoming evidence creates prediction errors.
- Precision weighting decides which errors matter and which are dismissed as noise.
- Action then helps confirm or defend the current model unless revision pressure is strong enough.

## Source-Grounded Example
In belief operations, a student who predicts "I am a C student" does not just hold an opinion. That model shapes attention, effort, interpretation, and avoidance. Contradictory evidence often gets explained away unless the prediction error is strong, attended to, and paired with a workable replacement model.

## Diagnostic/Use
Ask: "What prediction is organizing this person's experience right now, and what would have to happen for the prediction error to get real weight?"

Use this note when designing interventions, audits, or educational sequences that need genuine revision rather than surface agreement.

## Trap
Treating predictive processing as a total explanation for everything. Its value in the atlas is practical: it explains why evidence alone often fails and why revision needs attention, precision, and replacement structure.
