# Veil of Ignorance Fairness Case

> Better rules emerge when the rule-maker cannot preselect their own position inside the system.

## Definition
The veil-of-ignorance case turns fairness into a thought experiment: design the rule without knowing which place in the system you will occupy.

## Pattern
- A system must distribute power, cost, or protection.
- Self-interest distorts judgment.
- Identity position is temporarily removed from the decision.
- The resulting rule better reflects general fairness than factional advantage.

## Source-Grounded Example
The transcript uses Rawls's veil of ignorance to ask what kind of society a person would design if they did not know whether they would end up rich or poor, advantaged or vulnerable.

## Diagnostic/Use
Ask: "Would I still endorse this rule if I might occupy the worst-positioned slot under it?"

Use this case for hiring policy, compensation rules, institutional design, and leadership decisions that create winners and losers.

## Trap
Using fairness simulation to avoid all tradeoffs. The case clarifies justice pressure; it does not remove scarcity or conflict.
