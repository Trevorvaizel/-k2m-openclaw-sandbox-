# John Bogle Don't Lose Case

> In crowded competitive systems, removing structural loss can beat chasing brilliance.

## Definition
The John Bogle case shows inversion at work in markets: stop asking how to beat everyone and start asking how to stop losing to fees, friction, and false superiority.

## Pattern
- A field glamorizes outperforming the crowd.
- Data shows most participants fail after costs.
- The problem is inverted from "how do I win big?" to "how do I stop bleeding?"
- Structural loss removal becomes the advantage.

## Source-Grounded Example
The transcript describes Bogle looking at active investing and concluding that the real enemy was cost. By reducing fees close to zero, the investor beats most competitors who are trying to look brilliant.

## Diagnostic/Use
Ask: "What recurring loss is making the system unwinnable before I even start optimizing?"

Use this case for investing, operations, pricing, and any domain where friction compounds against the user.

## Trap
Confusing disciplined loss removal with passivity. The point is not to do nothing; it is to remove the structural leak first.
