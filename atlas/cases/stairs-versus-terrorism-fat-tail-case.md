# Stairs Versus Terrorism Fat Tail Case

> Average frequency is not enough when the shape of the downside is different.

## Definition
The stairs-versus-terrorism case shows why a bell-curve problem and a fat-tail problem cannot be judged with the same map.

## Pattern
- Two risks are compared using raw incident counts.
- One risk is independent and bounded.
- The other is systemic and capable of extreme tail events.
- A frequency-only comparison produces the wrong policy intuition.

## Source-Grounded Example
The transcript contrasts many ordinary stair deaths with fewer terrorism deaths, then argues that the comparison fails because stair incidents are bounded while a terrorist event can produce extreme systemic damage.

## Diagnostic/Use
Ask: "Am I comparing average frequency across territories that have different tail behavior?"

Use this case for security, infrastructure, market risk, and project planning where outlier downside matters more than average occurrence.

## Trap
Using data without asking what distribution generated it.
