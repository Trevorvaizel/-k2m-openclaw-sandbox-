# Jane Jacobs Statistical City Case

> Powerful maps can damage territory when they are imposed instead of tested.

## Definition
The Jane Jacobs statistical-city case shows the danger of forcing a living system to obey a clean model. A planning map can look rational while destroying the local complexity that makes the territory work.

## Pattern
- A planner builds a clean model of a messy living system.
- The mess is treated as error rather than signal.
- Power is used to reshape the territory around the model.
- The imposed order destroys functions the map did not understand.

## Source-Grounded Example
The transcript contrasts mid-century planning models of towers, empty green space, separated zones, and highways with Jane Jacobs's observation of street life, mixed use, and eyes on the street as the real safety system of vibrant neighborhoods.

## Diagnostic/Use
Ask: "What living function would this clean model destroy if we imposed it?"

Use this case when a strategy, organization chart, city plan, funnel, or operating system is being simplified before the territory has been observed.

## Trap
Romanticizing messiness. The case does not say disorder is always good; it says local function must be understood before cleanup becomes intervention.
