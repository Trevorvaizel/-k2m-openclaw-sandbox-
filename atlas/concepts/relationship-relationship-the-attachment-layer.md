# Relationship: The Attachment Layer

## Definition
John Bowlby's attachment theory and Mary Ainsworth's research on attachment styles identified four patterns that originate in infancy and operate as templates for adult intimate relationships:

## Pattern
- Secure: Comfortable with closeness and autonomy. Able to depend on others and be depended on. Can tolerate uncertainty in the relationship without catastrophising.
- Anxious (Preoccupied): Hyperactivated attachment system. The relationship is experienced as perpetually at risk. Constant monitoring for signs of withdrawal. Pursuer behaviour in conflict.
- Avoidant (Dismissive): Deactivated attachment system. Independence presented as self-sufficiency, but rooted in the learned expectation that closeness leads to disappointment. Withdrawer behaviour in conflict.
- Disorganised (Fearful): The partner is simultaneously the source of threat and the sought-for source of comfort. Often associated with early relational trauma. The person who both desperately wants closeness and finds it terrifying.
- Amir Levine and Rachel Heller (in *Attached*) demonstrated that these styles are not fixed character traits. They are relational strategies â€” and they interact predictably. The anxious-avoidant pairing is the most common and the most painful: the more the anxious partner pursues, the more the avoidant withdraws; the more the avoidant withdraws, the more the anxious partner pursues. Neither person is wrong. Both are running childhood-calibrated programs on adult hardware.
