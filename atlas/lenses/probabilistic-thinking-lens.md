# Probabilistic Thinking Lens

> Replace absolute predictions with weighted bets that update as reality arrives.

## Definition
The probabilistic-thinking lens treats judgment as calibration under uncertainty. Instead of forcing reality into yes-or-no stories, it starts with base rates, updates with new evidence, and asks what kind of distribution the territory actually has.

## Pattern
- The mind overreacts to vivid new information or an attractive narrative.
- A prior or base rate restores background context.
- New evidence updates the prior rather than replacing it automatically.
- Distribution shape, downside asymmetry, and repeat exposure determine the right action.

## Source-Grounded Example
The transcript moves from Bayesian priors to Vera Atkins's wartime judgment, fat-tail reasoning, the Linda problem, and Lloyd's of London. The common move is the same: judge uncertainty as a betting problem, not a drama problem.

## Diagnostic/Use
Ask: "What is the base rate, how strong is the new evidence, and what happens if I am wrong repeatedly?"

Use this lens for forecasting, hiring, intelligence, investing, risk design, and any decision where narrative confidence exceeds the actual information available.

## Trap
Performing statistical theater with fake precision. If the prior is invented, the evidence quality is ignored, or the distribution type is wrong, the numbers only decorate a bad map.
