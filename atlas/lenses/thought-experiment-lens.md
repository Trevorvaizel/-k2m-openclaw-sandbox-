# Thought Experiment Lens

> Run a disciplined simulation before reality charges the cost of learning.

## Definition
The thought-experiment lens uses imagination with rigor: question, hypothesis, mental test, analysis, and adjustment. It lets a user explore impossible, risky, ethical, or high-cost scenarios before acting.

## Pattern
- The user frames a scenario that cannot or should not be tested directly.
- The scenario is simulated under explicit constraints.
- The simulation reveals values, causal sensitivity, necessary conditions, or limits of prediction.
- The user treats the result as insight, not proof.

## Source-Grounded Example
The transcript uses Einstein's elevator to show how a disciplined simulation can reveal a hidden equivalence. It also uses Rawls's veil of ignorance and the Russian-roulette example to show that thought experiments help with fairness design and with separating process quality from lucky outcomes.

## Diagnostic/Use
Ask: "What can I learn safely in simulation before I make the real-world move?"

Use this lens for ethics, strategy, product decisions, and counterfactuals where direct testing is costly or impossible.

## Trap
Confusing a thought experiment with proof. The transcript stresses that history and complex systems can be too chaotic for clean alternate-past certainty.
