# Hanlon's Razor Lens

> Start by testing error, ignorance, or incentive before jumping to intent.

## Definition
Hanlon's razor is a social-probability lens. It says that many harmful outcomes are better explained by mistake, confusion, distraction, or misaligned incentive than by calculated malice.

## Pattern
- A harmful event produces an immediate villain story.
- The mind overweights intent because intent is narratively satisfying.
- A simpler explanation such as error or entropy is checked first.
- Repeated evidence can still justify moving from mistake to malice.

## Source-Grounded Example
The transcript uses road rage, the Linda problem, and later the Ponzi-scheme boundary to show how the lens works. Start by assuming mistake, but update when specific repeated evidence of malice appears.

## Diagnostic/Use
Ask: "What boring mistake or incentive explanation fits this before I assign hostile intent?"

Use this lens for management, relationships, conflict diagnosis, and email or message interpretation where narrative escalation is cheap and costly conflict is one step away.

## Trap
Turning Hanlon's razor into naive forgiveness. It is a starting posture for diagnosis, not a command to ignore patterns of deliberate abuse or deception.
