# Logical-Level Force

> Applying the right map to the wrong level produces untraceable confusion.

## Definition
The Logical-Level Force is the invisible pull toward applying a map calibrated at one logical level to phenomena that operate at a different level. The result is not correctable error — it is paradox and double-bind, because the tools available at Level I cannot resolve problems that live at Level II or III.

## Surface Pattern
A cartographer applies a force or framework that worked at one level of abstraction — say, correcting an action — to a problem that lives at the level of the governing premises that generate those actions. The intervention fails inexplicably. The framework is right; the level is wrong.

## Invisible Mechanism
Maps are calibrated at specific logical levels. Bateson distinguished at least three: Learning I (changing response within a given set of alternatives), Learning II (change in the process of Learning I — revising which alternatives are considered), and Learning III (change in the premises governing Learning II — dissolving the identity that was built around the map). Each level has its own causal logic. Level I tools produce untraceable confusion when applied to Level II or III problems. Bateson called the deepest form of this failure a "double-bind": commands that operate at different logical types simultaneously, making any response wrong.

## Second-Order Force
Level-crossing failures are diagnosed as practitioner failure, not map failure. The cartographer applies the correct tool at the wrong level and receives inexplicable results. Diagnosis stays trapped inside the wrong level's vocabulary. The more rigorous the tool, the more confidently the wrong level gets addressed.

## Application
Before applying any force or framework, ask: is this calibrated for visible action, for method revision, or for premise revision? If the symptom lives at one level but the map addresses another, reframe before acting. The Decommissioning Force (Force XI) and the Map-Duplicity Force (Force XIII) operate at Level III — revising governing premises — and cannot be activated by Level I or II tools alone.

## Failure Mode
The manifesto contains forces of different logical levels presented as peers. The practitioner cannot use them simultaneously without inducing contradictions. Deep revision fails because decommissioning tools operate at a level below the maps they are trying to retire. The cartographer mistakes the persistence of the problem for practitioner failure.
