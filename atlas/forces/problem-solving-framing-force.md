# Framing Force

> The first mistake is often made before the first solution.

## Definition
The framing force is the power of a problem statement to define the search space, the evidence that counts, the people invited in, and the actions that remain thinkable.

## Pattern
- a problem statement is never neutral
- naming the problem one way hides other causes and other interventions
- most teams rush to solutions before asking what kind of problem has actually been named
- whoever controls the frame often controls the range of acceptable action

## Source-Grounded Example
The manifesto contrasts frames like "revenue is down," "the category belief has collapsed," and "this is a trust problem disguised as a pricing problem" to show how different frames generate different solution spaces.

## Diagnostic / Use
Ask:
- What problem has been named?
- What problem has been hidden?
- Who benefits from the current frame?
- What alternative frame would enlarge the intervention space?

## Trap or Limitation
Infinite reframing without intervention. The point of a better frame is to produce better action, not endless abstraction.
