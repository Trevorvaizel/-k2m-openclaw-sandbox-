# Decommissioning Force

> The map that cannot be killed becomes the ceiling.

## Definition
The Decommissioning Force is the invisible pull that preserves outdated maps past their explanatory usefulness. Maps generate cognitive authority proportional to their elegance and the effort invested in building them. The more beautiful the map, the harder it is to retire even after it stops explaining reality.

## Surface Pattern
Force names accumulate without pruning. Old maps persist after their explanatory power fades. Cartographers defend their best-loved concepts against contrary evidence, citing the effort already invested.

## Invisible Mechanism
Cognitive authority and sunk-cost attachment work together. A map that has explained reality reliably acquires trust. That trust persists even when the territory changes. Deep domain mastery amplifies this — the same expertise that produces strong maps builds strong resistance to their revision. Elegant language adds another layer: beautiful formulations feel like they must be true.

## Second-Order Force
Stale maps block new territory contact. The cartographer frozen in old maps produces more artifacts in the same register instead of updating perception. At scale, the canon becomes archaeology — preserving prior thinking instead of current reality.

## Application
Institutionalize map killing. The Annual Destruction practice (Tool 11) makes decommissioning a recurring discipline rather than a crisis response. The Associative Barrier Audit (Tool 13) catches barriers before they calcify. Steelman opposition before treating any force as settled.

## Failure Mode
The manifesto or atlas accumulates maps that no longer explain what they claim to explain. New territory arrives; old maps are applied anyway. Practitioners cite the canon as authority while the territory diverges.
