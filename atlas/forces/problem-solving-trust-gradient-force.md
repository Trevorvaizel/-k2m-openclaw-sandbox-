# Problem-Solving Trust Gradient Force

> Truth does not travel if the receiving system does not trust the diagnosis enough to move.

## Definition
The problem-solving trust gradient is the sequence by which diagnosis, evidence, and intervention become believable enough across stakeholders to permit real action.

## Pattern
- the best diagnosis still stalls when the trust layer is missing
- a blocked intervention may be socially unbelievable rather than analytically wrong
- trust determines who can name the problem, who can surface evidence, and who can cause change
- many "implementation problems" are really trust-translation problems

## Source-Grounded Example
The manifesto repeatedly asks what layer of trust has not yet been built and treats trust as one of the invisible forces governing whether people can act on truth.

## Diagnostic / Use
Ask:
- Who must trust this diagnosis before movement is possible?
- What evidence is persuasive in this environment?
- Where is the choke point: credibility, legitimacy, incentives, or fear?
- Is the intervention being rejected because it is false or because it has not crossed the trust gradient?

## Trap or Limitation
Treating trust as softness. In hard systems, trust is often part of the mechanism, not a cosmetic overlay.
