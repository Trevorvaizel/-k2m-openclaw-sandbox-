# Map-Duplicity Force

> The map a practitioner claims to use and the map their actions reveal are rarely the same map.

## Definition
The Map-Duplicity Force is the invisible gap between two maps a practitioner holds simultaneously: the espoused theory (the map they claim governs their work, serving identity and social functions) and the theory-in-use (the map their actual decisions reveal, governing real behavior). The gap is maintained by cognitive and organizational defensive routines that prevent the data that would expose it from being discussed.

## Surface Pattern
A practitioner articulates a model they say governs their work. Observing them in action reveals a different model operating. They are not lying — they are genuinely unaware of the gap. Cartographic research based on self-report captures the espoused map and publishes it as the field's operative structure.

## Invisible Mechanism
Practitioners hold two maps simultaneously. The espoused theory is available for articulation — it is the map they have consciously developed and are willing to defend. The theory-in-use is implicit in their action sequence — it is what their decisions, timing, attention, and omissions actually reveal. These maps may share vocabulary while diverging completely on operative rules. The espoused map serves social display; the theory-in-use generates actual results. The gap is protected by defensive routines — organizational antibodies that prevent the data that would expose the gap from being surfaced or discussed.

## Second-Order Force
Cartographic research conducted through self-report produces a false artifact. The cartographer surfaces the espoused map and publishes it as the field's structure. The theory-in-use — which generates actual success, failure, and expertise — remains invisible. Practitioners trained on the espoused map cannot replicate expert performance because they are learning a social performance, not an operative system. At scale, the canon captures what practitioners wish were true, not what generates results.

## Application
Extract theory-in-use through case-based inquiry, not self-report. Ask practitioners to walk through a specific decision: what happened, what did you notice, why did you do this and not that? Compare the stated reasons with the action sequence. The gap between them is often where the field's actual operative logic lives. The Expert Contrast Interview (Tool 4) uses contrast and anomaly for this reason — they pressure the gap into visibility.

## Failure Mode
The manifesto captures what experts say they do rather than what they do. It becomes the field's aspirational self-description — elegant, plausible, and inert. Practitioners who follow it fail in the same ways practitioners always failed because the operative map was never surfaced.
