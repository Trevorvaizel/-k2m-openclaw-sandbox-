## New Knowledge
- Charlie Munger's lattice idea makes mental models useful together; single-model thinking creates blind spots.
- Wisdom is framed as finding the right solution for the right problem, and often avoiding the problem before it exists.
- Mental models should stay grounded in reality; ideas lose power when they detach from the territory.
- The first five trial lenses are map/territory, circle of competence, first principles, thought experiments, and second-order thinking.
- Stories are not trivia. They are case evidence that makes a lens operational.
- Map usefulness depends on purpose-fit: the structure it preserves must match the job the user is trying to do.
- Every map should trigger a cartographer audit: who drew it, what they wanted, and what they omitted.
- Good models have boundary conditions; field evidence should update or constrain them.
- Powerful maps can act back on territory when leaders impose the model instead of observing the living system.
- Competence is defined by boundary awareness: knowing what you can judge, what you cannot, and when you are only holding surface familiarity.
- A circle of competence expands through learning, recorded track records, and outside feedback, not through confidence alone.
- Borrowed competence is useful only when the guide is real and their incentives are visible.
- Leadership beyond direct expertise requires structure for disagreement and synthesis, not pretense of omniscience.
- First-principles thinking is not just a stance; it needs disciplined methods such as Socratic questioning and the five whys.
- A first principle is context-specific and must survive falsifiability pressure rather than authority or historical habit.
- Method worship is a recurrent failure mode: people copy the curved chute and forget the low-stress principle underneath.
- Long successful trends can hide hostile underlying mechanisms; the turkey problem is a warning against treating backtests as laws.
- Thought experiments are useful when direct testing is impossible, dangerous, or ethically constrained, but they are still maps rather than proof.
- Process quality and outcome quality diverge. A lucky outcome can conceal a bad process, which is why Russian-roulette thinking is diagnostically useful.
- Second-order thinking is incentive literacy. Many system failures are not caused by bad goals but by the adaptive behavior induced by the intervention.
- Probabilistic thinking starts with base rates and then updates. It is closer to betting than predicting.
- Fat-tail territories cannot be judged with bell-curve intuitions. Distribution shape changes strategy.
- Occam's razor and Hanlon's razor are probability filters that reduce narrative error, not universal laws that end inquiry.
- Inversion is strongest when made operational: define the anti-goal, surface the failure path, remove the parking brake, and guard against zeros.
- A latticework is not a pile of facts. It is a cross-disciplinary structure of models that stays useful only while tested against the ground.
